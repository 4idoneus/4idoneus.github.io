Electromagnetic Interference (EMI)

Back to Issues List Get in Touch

In an industrial or office environment, data cables (e.g., Cat5e/6 UTP) are run in close proximity to heavy machinery with large motors, fluorescent lighting ballasts, or high-voltage power conduits. The strong electromagnetic fields generated by these devices induce electrical noise onto the copper conductors within the data cables. This EMI corrupts the delicate electrical signals transmitting data, manifesting as intermittent network disconnections, significantly slower data transfer speeds, frequent data retransmissions (due to corrupted packets), and even complete communication failures for devices in that area. The electrical noise essentially "drowns out" or distorts the legitimate data signals.

Shielded Twisted Pair (STP) and Fiber Optic cables are specifically designed to counteract or be immune to EMI.

Shielded Twisted Pair (STP): STP cables incorporate a metallic shield (either a foil wrap, a braided mesh, or both) around the twisted pairs of wires. This metallic shield acts as a Faraday cage, effectively blocking external electromagnetic fields from penetrating and interfering with the internal data-carrying conductors. The twisted pairs within the shield still reduce internal crosstalk, and the shield provides an additional layer of protection against external noise. Proper grounding of the shield is critical to drain away induced currents.
Fiber Optic Cable: Fiber optic cables transmit data using pulses of light through glass or plastic strands. Since they do not use electrical signals, they are completely immune to all forms of electromagnetic interference, radio frequency interference (RFI), and electrical surges. This makes them the ideal solution in environments with extremely high levels of electromagnetic noise, in medical facilities where electrical isolation is critical, or for long-distance data transmission where signal degradation over copper would be an issue.

  1. Reroute Cables for Physical Separation: The first and often most cost-effective step is to physically relocate the existing cables. Identify the primary sources of EMI (e.g., specific machinery, power conduits, fluorescent light fixtures). Plan new cable runs that maximize the physical distance between the data cables and these EMI sources. This might involve using different cable trays, separate conduit systems, or routing cables through different walls, ceilings, or even different sides of a building.
  2. Replace UTP with STP or Fiber Optic Cables: For areas where physical rerouting isn't feasible or sufficient to eliminate EMI, replace the unshielded twisted pair (UTP) cables with appropriate shielded solutions.
    If the EMI is moderate, upgrade to Shielded Twisted Pair (STP) cables (e.g., Cat6a STP). Ensure that proper grounding practices are followed for the STP cabling system, as the shield needs to be correctly grounded at one or both ends (depending on the specific standard and environment) to effectively drain away induced currents.
    If the EMI is severe, or if long-distance, extremely high-bandwidth, and absolute immunity to electrical noise are paramount, deploy Fiber Optic Cables. This transition typically requires installing media converters (to convert electrical signals from network devices to optical signals and vice-versa) at both ends of the fiber run.
  3. Test with Advanced Cable Testers and Network Performance Monitoring: After implementing the solution, use a certified network cable tester (e.g., Fluke Networks Versiv, LanTEK) to perform comprehensive tests. These testers can verify not only basic continuity and wire map but also advanced metrics like noise margin, signal-to-noise ratio (SNR), and alien crosstalk. A good tester can detect ambient electromagnetic noise and validate the cable's performance in the presence of potential interference. Additionally, monitor actual network performance metrics on the affected devices (e.g., ping response times, file transfer speeds, packet loss rates, retransmission counts) to confirm that the connectivity issues have been resolved and the network is stable and performing reliably.
