Improper Cable Termination

Back to Issues List Get in Touch

A network technician manually crimps an Ethernet cable (e.g., Cat5e or Cat6) to connect a new workstation to the network. During the crimping process, the technician either mixes up the wire order, fails to untwist the pairs minimally, or doesn't push the wires completely to the end of the RJ45 connector, or doesn't seat the cable jacket properly under the strain relief. When this improperly terminated cable is plugged in, the network interface card (NIC) on the workstation might report "no link," or if a link is established, it's often highly unstable. This instability manifests as frequent disconnections, significantly high latency, severe packet loss, and extremely slow data transfer speeds. This is because the incorrect wire order disrupts the delicate balance of the twisted pairs (leading to excessive crosstalk and signal reflections), prevents proper electrical contact with the connector pins, and interferes with the correct signaling required for Ethernet communication.

Correct cable termination according to a standard like T568B (or T568A) is fundamental for the physical layer's integrity.

Maintains Twisted Pair Integrity: Ethernet relies on twisted pairs to cancel out electromagnetic interference and crosstalk. The T568B standard ensures that these twists are maintained as close as possible to the RJ45 connector, minimizing signal degradation.
Correct Signal Assignment: Each of the 8 pins on an RJ45 connector has a specific function for Ethernet communication (e.g., transmit positive, transmit negative, receive positive, receive negative). The standard dictates which wire from which pair connects to which pin, ensuring that network devices can correctly send and receive data without confusion or short circuits.
Impedance Matching: Proper termination helps maintain consistent impedance across the cable and into the connector. Mismatched impedance causes signal reflections, which lead to errors and reduced performance.
Reliable Physical Connection: When wires are properly seated and crimped, they make solid electrical contact with the connector pins and the cable jacket provides essential strain relief, preventing wires from pulling out. Adherence to the standard ensures universal compatibility and reliable electrical signal flow, preventing data errors and connection instability.

  1. Strip and Re-crimp Both Ends (or the faulty end): Begin by carefully cutting off the improperly crimped RJ45 connector(s) using a proper cable cutting tool. Then, using a specialized network cable stripper, remove just enough of the cable's outer jacket (typically 0.5 to 0.75 inches, depending on the connector) to expose the four twisted pairs of wires. Exercise caution to avoid nicking the insulation of the inner wires.
  2. Use the Correct Wire Order (T568B Standard): Carefully untwist the exposed wire pairs only minimally, just enough to straighten them. Then, arrange the individual wires precisely according to the T568B standard color code from left to right, ensuring the RJ45 connector's clip is facing away from you and the gold pins are facing up:
    In case you forgot : T568B Standard

    The T568B standard is one of two wiring standards (the other being T568A) that defines the order of the individual wires within an 8-position RJ45 modular connector for twisted-pair Ethernet cables. It's the most widely used standard for new commercial and residential network installations, especially in the United States.

    When you're terminating an Ethernet cable (like Cat5e, Cat6, Cat6a) with an RJ45 connector, you need to arrange the eight individual wires in a specific sequence before crimping them into the connector. The T568B standard dictates this sequence from Pin 1 to Pin 8 when holding the RJ45 connector with the clip facing away from you and the pins facing up:

    1. White/Orange
    2. Orange
    3. White/Green
    4. Blue
    5. White/Blue
    6. Green
    7. White/Brown
    8. Brown

    Why this order is important:

    • Signal Integrity: Ethernet uses twisted pairs to minimize electromagnetic interference (EMI) and crosstalk. The specific order in T568B ensures that the correct wire from each twisted pair (e.g., Orange and White/Orange) is connected to the right pins for transmit and receive signals, maintaining the integrity of these twists right up to the connector.
    • Compatibility: Following a standard ensures that your cables are compatible with network devices (computers, switches, routers) from various manufacturers, allowing them to communicate effectively.
    • Straight-Through Cables: For a "straight-through" Ethernet cable (used to connect dissimilar devices like a computer to a switch), both ends of the cable are typically wired using the same standard (e.g., T568B on both ends).
    • Crossover Cables: While less common with modern auto-MDI/MDIX network equipment, "crossover" cables (used to connect similar devices like two computers directly) typically have one end wired to T568A and the other to T568B.

    In essence, T568B provides a consistent and reliable method for wiring Ethernet cables, which is fundamental for proper network operation.

    Once arranged in the correct order, trim the wires straight and to the correct length so they will all reach the end of the RJ45 connector. Carefully push all eight wires straight and firmly into the RJ45 connector until they hit the end, ensuring that the cable's outer jacket is also seated inside the connector for strain relief. Use a proper crimping tool to secure the wires and jacket in place.
  3. Test with a Cable Tester (Continuity and Wire Map): The most critical step is testing. Use a dedicated network cable tester (ranging from basic continuity testers to advanced certification testers).
    A basic continuity tester will confirm that all 8 wires have an end-to-end connection.
    A more advanced wire map tester is essential as it not only checks for continuity but also verifies the correct pin-to-pin correspondence (i.e., that Pin 1 on one end connects to Pin 1 on the other, Pin 2 to Pin 2, and so on, according to the standard). This type of tester will immediately highlight any crossed pairs, split pairs, shorts, or open circuits caused by improper termination, often with a visual display or sequence of lights. If a "fail" is indicated, meticulously recrimp the faulty end(s) until the tester shows a "pass" and confirms the correct wire map.
